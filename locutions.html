LOCUTIONS


I just found a synonymous term for tongue-twisters, and a shorter one. Anyway, a study says that the hardest tongue-twister is this: "Pad kid poured curd pulled cod.". In the English language, probably because they were from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology. But the article says "it is the world's most difficult." When I read this, I just thought to myself, "you haven't heard of a Polish tongue-twister before."

Polish tongue-twisters are without a doubt insane. Even Poles (at least one of those who I vocally talked with) have difficulties speaking my favorite tongue-twister, which is the first one on this list. Someone told me that she's a master of Polish tongue-twisters, so here's some 40+ challenges for you:

HARD (SOMEHOW EASY)

I'd like to say this set is easy, because that's normally how challenges begin, but if I did I will be lying that this is easy. So, here we go:





A BEETLE

Szczebrzeszynie chrząszcz brzmi w trzcinie i Szczebrzeszyn z tego słynie..

f shch'ebj(e)'eshi(h)ny'e hsho(n)shch bj(e)m'ee f tshch'eeny'e 'ee shch'ebj(e)'eshi(h)n z t'eg'o swi(h)ny'e.

In Szczebrzeszyn, the beetle sounds in the reed and Szczebrzeszyn is famous for it.



When I knew this place exists, I immediately put Szczebrzeszyn to my hope-to-visit-in-seven-years-places list.





YOU BEING HAPPY

ch'esh'ew(n) sh'ew(n) j(e)'e sh'ew(n) ch'eshi(h)sh.

ch'esh'ew(n) sh'ew(n) j(e)'e sh'ew(n) ch'eshi(h)sh

I'm glad you're happy.



I mean, how come a simple phrase is so tricky to say?!

THE BLACK BUN

shwi(h) phwi(h) p'o l'eeny'e. phw'a phw'ew(n) ph'awo(n), 'a ph'aw'a pw'ak'awo(n), j(e)'e phw'a phw'ew(n) ph'aw'a.

Siskin combed his black bun, cleaning each of its curls next he covered his bun with a toque, but part of his curls came out from the side.






VOCABULARY

I decided to put this part at the end, because I find it interesting that some Polish expressions are so common at one part of Poland, and unknown to others. Here are a few examples:

"No" means "yes" in Poland

There have been tons of videos about this in the internet, but it is still worth mentioning here. "No" in Poland literally means "well" or anything like that, because it is often used as a filler. It is pronounced "noh," like saying the English word "no" but suddenly stopping the sound before the nasal "ow" comes, making it sound somewhat hard. Here, have some Easy Polish, one of my sources in learning Polish.








"One" means "many" in Poland

Polish words are difficult to pronounce (a lot of them, as you already know by now). But they are read or pronounced the way they are written so it kind of reduces the difficulty of the language a bit. In this example, "one" in Polish is pronounced "oh-neh" meaning "they" (if the people being referred to have no males) in English, while "one" in English is pronounced "wuh-n" meaning "single" or "solitary." This is not a grammar website, but that is an interesting language exchange fact right there. You're welcome.

"Ę" is never at the beginning of any Polish wor...

I decided to put this part at the end, because I find it interesting that some Polish expressions are so common at one part of Poland, and unknown from others. The Polish character "ę" is like the "B" in German which cannot be in the beginning of a word, except for a rather 'unreal' word "ęą," which a friend told me about. Friends from Rzeszów, Sandomierz, and Kraków say that it is not a real word and they don't even know what it means. "Ęą" is basically a word with only two letters which are nasal ones, and pronounced "eh-wh(n)," which means "to get stuck."

It also appears that "ęsi" is also a word known to some Poles but not to many. It means "geese" ("gęsi" in Polish) like the plural of "goose." "Ęsi" is also similar to how the informal expression "siema" (means "hello") was created, so apparently this word "ęsi" is just a result of natives playing with the language.
